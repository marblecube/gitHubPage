SLO-MOTION DESTRUCTO

The sky glares;
angry, ready.

tap tap tap
go the big balls of water
as they dot the ground and

The sky changes colors.

Now, she is recollecting the time,
as a child,
when the ball knocked her mouth from out her face
and a single tooth laying upon the black top
poured smiles up into her eyes.

Now, the front right tire offers up its last millimeter of rubber and explodes,
spitting white threads and chunks of tattered midnight.
The rest squeal and collapse in upon themselves
gracefully tracing strands of onyxSpaghetti across moistening asphalt.

The electrical pole, in its grim and solitary, know-it-here-and-now reality,
possesses planes, layers, frames, opacities, and thicknesses.

And they (she and it) have been interdependent since before her mother’s mother carried
a bouquet of daises across an open field and the lumber mill began the dispensing of
tiny, white envelopes of money to the men.

The witness is close-by cleaning his responsibility
and day-dreaming of new shoes and polished book cases.

The tearing of the air, the microscopic churning of enslaved chemicals,
the ripping, whistling metal,
the black and brown wood, splintering

--he hears these now.

The seat belt flaps loosely by her side;
following for a short, quick inch as she pitches forward
and, with her head, bursts the windshield into
shimmering, lapus-lazuli jubilee.

In three and seventeen-twentyfourths seconds,
when he runs up, leaving wet footprints behind,
he will be exactly 1 minute and 12 seconds away from noticing
the she will never eat the orange that
is / are / will be
strewn
here and there.

For now, all he knows is that she is trying to scream
but only managing softly croaked bubbles of
bile, phlegm, spittle, and blood.

THE LATERALIZATION OF LT. CMDR. ALAN BEAN

Imagine this: You are being shaken violently by 7.5 million pounds of thrust pushing out of five F1 rocket engines somewhere behind you, and thirty six and a half seconds after lifting off from the Kennedy Space Center atop a Saturn V, every warning indicator on your control panel simultaneously lights up, fuel-cell disconnect flags pop, and your instrumentation malfunctions.

Seventeen and a half seconds later, the “8-ball” attitude indicator begins spinning wildly, the telemetry stream from Mission Control is garbled, and, since there aren’t any windows, it’s impossible for you to see what’s happening.

It’s November 14, 1969 and you are one of three astronauts on America’s Apollo 12 mission –the second mission in the history of humankind to venture towards the Moon.

Your entire life has been prelude to this moment.

The world is watching.

You are the spacecraft systems engineer.

And you have no idea what’s happening.

Spirit of Space Flight by Alan Bean shows a highly stylized pale green rocket lifting off with a grey and white background it is rendered in a tiled mosaic style.
